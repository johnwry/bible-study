# Chapter 12

## The Serious Church

The conversation slowed after that.

Not because they had run out of things to say.

But because what they had already discovered was beginning to settle in.

Adrián finally looked at Matías.

“You said you visited a church too.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Well?”

“Well what?”

“Your turn.”

Matías hesitated.

“I’m not sure it’s relevant.”

Adrián smiled.

“That’s exactly what I said.”

Marcos nodded.

“Tell us about it.”

Matías leaned back slightly, thinking.

“It was different.”

“How,” Elías asked.

“Very serious.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“That doesn’t narrow it down.”

“No,” Matías said quietly.

“I mean very serious.”

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“In what sense?”

Matías spoke slowly.

“The people were disciplined.”

“That’s good,” Adrián said.

“Yes.”

“The service was orderly.”

“That’s also good.”

“Yes.”

“There was a strong emphasis on obedience.”

Adrián nodded.

“Still sounds good.”

Matías continued.

“They talked a lot about holiness.”

“That’s definitely good,” Adrián said.

“Yes.”

“But the atmosphere felt…”

He paused.

“Heavy.”

Marcos tilted his head.

“Explain.”

Matías thought for a moment.

“It felt like everyone was trying very hard.”

“Trying how?” Elías asked.

“To live up to something.”

Adrián smiled.

“That sounds like church.”

Matías didn’t smile.

“It felt more intense than that.”

“How so?” Marcos asked.

“There was constant talk about discipline.”

“Daily devotions.”

“Accountability.”

“Guarding your life carefully.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“That sounds very committed.”

“Yes.”

“No one seemed casual about their faith.”

“That’s admirable,” Elías said.

“Yes,” Matías replied.

“But there was also something else.”

“What,” Marcos asked.

Matías took a moment before answering.

“Pressure.”

The others looked at him.

“Pressure?”

“Yes.”

“To measure up.”

Adrián frowned slightly.

“To what?”

“To the standard.”

“What standard?”

Matías shrugged slightly.

“Holiness.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“Was Scripture being taught?”

“Yes.”

“Very directly.”

“Then what felt wrong?” Adrián asked.

Matías hesitated again.

“I’m not sure anything was wrong.”

The table went quiet.

Marcos spoke first.

“Sometimes things feel wrong before we know why.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“What kind of teaching was emphasized?”

Matías answered.

“Obedience.”

“Self-discipline.”

“Guarding your life carefully.”

“That doesn’t sound unbiblical,” Marcos said.

“No,” Matías said.

“But the message seemed to return to the same point every time.”

“What point?” Elías asked.

“That the Christian life requires constant effort.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That’s not exactly shocking.”

“No,” Matías said.

“But it felt like something else was missing.”

“What.”

Matías looked down at the table.

“I’m not sure.”

The others waited.

Finally Marcos asked,

“How did people relate to each other?”

Matías thought about it.

“They were polite.”

“Respectful.”

“But…”

“But what?” Adrián asked.

“There was distance.”

“Distance?”

“Yes.”

“It felt like everyone was watching themselves.”

“Watching themselves?” Elías said.

“Yes.”

“Careful not to fail.”

The table was quiet again.

Adrián finally said,

“That sounds exhausting.”

Matías didn’t respond.

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“You know what this reminds me of.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“The church in Galatia.”

Matías looked up.

“Why.”

Marcos answered.

“Because Paul said they began by the Spirit…”

He paused.

“…but were trying to finish by the flesh.”

Elías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

Adrián looked between them.

“Are you saying a church could become very serious about obedience…”

He gestured toward Matías.

“…and still be operating in the flesh?”

Elías spoke carefully.

“That’s exactly what Paul said about Galatia.”

Matías sat quietly.

Finally he spoke.

“That’s difficult to accept.”

“Why,” Adrián asked.

Matías answered slowly.

“Because if effort can be flesh…”

He paused.

“Then the problem might be much closer than we think.”

No one answered.

Because each of them had just realized something.

Carnality might not only appear in lively churches like Corinth.

It might also appear in churches that were extremely serious about doing what was right.

And that possibility made the investigation far more personal than any of them expected.
