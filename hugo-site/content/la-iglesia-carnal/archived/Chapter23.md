# Chapter 23

## The Cross Does Not Bow to Carnality

The next evening they met again.

No one suggested a different topic.

The direction of the conversation had already been decided.

Adrián arrived first as usual.

But he wasn’t talking at the counter.

He was sitting at the table, quietly stirring his coffee.

Matías noticed it immediately.

“You look thoughtful.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That happens occasionally.”

Matías sat down across from him.

“I’ve been thinking about what we said yesterday.”

“The cross?”

“Yes.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“Me too.”

Elías arrived next, Bible already in hand.

Marcos came in last.

No one wasted time.

Adrián finally spoke.

“So if the cross is the answer…”

He paused.

“…then we need to understand what it actually does.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Paul seems to connect the cross with something very specific.”

“What,” Matías asked.

“The flesh.”

Elías turned quickly to Galatians again.

“There’s a passage.”

Adrián smiled.

“There’s always a passage.”

Elías read slowly.

> “But may it never be that I would boast, except in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ…”

He paused briefly.

Then finished the sentence.

> “…through which the world has been crucified to me, and I to the world.”

The table was quiet.

Matías spoke first.

“That’s strong language.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“Crucifixion is not a gentle idea.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“What exactly is Paul saying there?”

Elías answered carefully.

“He’s saying something decisive happened.”

“What kind of thing?”

“A separation.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“The world no longer governs him.”

Adrián looked thoughtful.

“That’s interesting.”

“What is?” Marcos asked.

“Because crucifixion isn’t negotiation.”

The others looked at him.

Adrián continued.

“It’s final.”

The word sat quietly on the table.

Final.

Elías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“And Paul speaks about it as something already accomplished.”

Marcos added,

“The cross did not merely advise the flesh.”

“It ended its authority.”

Matías leaned back slightly.

“That’s very different from how churches often approach carnality.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“Most systems try to control it.”

“Balance it.”

“Manage it.”

Marcos spoke quietly.

“But the cross does something else.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“It exposes it.”

The table went quiet again.

Elías turned to another passage.

“Paul says something similar in Romans.”

He read slowly.

> “Our old self was crucified with Him…”

Adrián frowned slightly.

“Crucified again.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

Matías spoke carefully.

“That language is absolute.”

“Yes.”

“Crucifixion doesn’t leave room for improvement.”

Marcos nodded.

“It declares an end.”

Adrián leaned back.

“So the cross does not cooperate with the flesh.”

“No.”

“It does not train it.”

“No.”

“It does not refine it.”

“No.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“It condemns it.”

The café noise continued softly around them.

But the table had grown still.

Matías spoke next.

“That explains something.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Why the message of the cross always feels offensive.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Because it does not flatter human effort.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That’s true.”

“It removes it.”

Elías looked down at the passage again.

“That’s exactly why Paul said the word of the cross is foolishness to many.”

“Why?” Matías asked.

“Because people naturally want something else.”

“What.”

Adrián answered.

“Improvement.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“People want the flesh corrected.”

“Strengthened.”

“Disciplined.”

“But the cross does something far more radical.”

The others waited.

Marcos finished the thought quietly.

“It declares the flesh finished.”

No one spoke for a moment.

Because that was not how most of them had thought about it.

Adrián finally said what everyone was realizing.

“So the cross doesn’t bow to carnality.”

“No,” Elías said.

“It stands against it.”

Matías spoke carefully.

“That would explain something else.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Why churches sometimes soften the message.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“They emphasize effort.”

“Growth.”

“Discipline.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“But not crucifixion.”

“No,” Elías said.

“Because crucifixion removes human pride.”

The table grew quiet again.

Adrián finally said it out loud.

“Maybe that’s why Paul refused to leave the cross.”

The others looked at him.

He continued.

“If carnality is the problem…”

He gestured lightly toward the Bible.

“…then the cross is the only message that actually confronts it.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“And that means something important.”

“What,” Matías asked.

“The cross will never adjust itself to fit carnality.”

The words settled heavily over the table.

Adrián looked down at his coffee.

“So if a church builds its life around controlling the flesh…”

He paused.

“…the cross will always disturb that system.”

No one disagreed.

Because they were beginning to see something clearly.

The cross does not bow.

It does not soften.

It does not negotiate with the flesh.

It declares its end.

And that is exactly why it is both feared…

and powerful.
