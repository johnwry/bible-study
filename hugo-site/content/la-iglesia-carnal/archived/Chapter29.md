# Chapter 29

## Grace Has Made the Difference

The conversation that evening began slowly.

Not because the men had nothing to say.

But because they had said so much already.

For days they had traced the problem of carnality through the Scriptures.

Corinth had shown them carnality in open immorality.

Galatia had shown them carnality hidden behind moral seriousness.

Ephesus had revealed carnality disguised as doctrinal devotion.

Laodicea had exposed carnality thriving in religious success.

And every path had eventually led them back to the same place.

The cross.

Adrián spoke first.

“So if everything we’ve said is true…”

He paused.

“…then the real difference must be grace.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“But not the way people usually talk about grace.”

Marcos leaned forward.

“What do you mean?”

Matías thought for a moment.

“Grace is often treated like forgiveness.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“Something that helps when we fail.”

Elías added quietly,

“A safety net.”

Marcos looked down at the open Bible.

“But Paul speaks of grace very differently.”

Adrián raised an eyebrow.

“How?”

Marcos turned a few pages and read aloud.

> “For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation to all men…”

He paused.

Then continued.

> “…instructing us to deny ungodliness and worldly desires…”

The others leaned closer.

Marcos finished the sentence.

> “…and to live sensibly, righteously and godly in the present age.”

The words lingered for a moment.

Adrián spoke softly.

“That sounds active.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Grace is not passive.”

Elías added quietly,

“It actually teaches.”

Marcos looked thoughtful.

“So grace doesn’t excuse carnality.”

“No,” Matías said.

“It replaces it.”

Adrián leaned back.

“That changes the entire picture.”

“How?” Elías asked.

“If grace is only forgiveness…”

Adrián shrugged.

“…then the Christian life becomes constant failure.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Fall.”

“Confess.”

“Start again.”

Matías spoke slowly.

“But if grace is the power of God…”

“…then something deeper is happening.”

The others looked at him.

“What do you mean?” Adrián asked.

Matías gestured toward the Bible.

“Grace doesn’t merely pardon the old life.”

He paused.

“It produces a new one.”

The table fell quiet again.

Elías finally spoke.

“That would explain why Paul speaks so confidently.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“Sometimes almost shockingly so.”

Marcos smiled slightly.

“Like Romans.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Yes.”

“Romans.”

He quoted from memory.

> “Sin shall not be master over you, for you are not under law but under grace.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“That statement only makes sense if grace actually changes something.”

Elías leaned forward slightly.

“It removes the entire framework of carnality management.”

Marcos added,

“And replaces it with life in the Spirit.”

Adrián looked around the table.

“So the gospel was never meant to produce controlled carnality.”

The others shook their heads.

“No.”

“It produces something else.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“A different kind of life.”

Elías nodded.

“Yes.”

“A life that flows from the cross.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“That explains something.”

“What?” Marcos asked.

“Why the New Testament speaks so often about fruit.”

Matías smiled.

“Yes.”

“Fruit.”

“Not effort.”

“Not systems.”

“Fruit.”

Elías added quietly,

“And fruit grows from life.”

The café noise continued around them.

But the table had grown still once again.

Adrián finally spoke.

“You know what surprises me the most.”

“What?” Matías asked.

“That this solution was sitting in front of us the entire time.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“The cross.”

“The gospel.”

“Grace.”

Elías smiled faintly.

“And we kept looking somewhere else.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Trying to solve the problem.”

Matías nodded.

“When the answer had already been given.”

The four men sat quietly for a moment.

Because the conversation had reached a place they had not expected when it began.

Not a new system.

Not a better strategy.

But something far simpler.

And far more powerful.

Adrián finally said it out loud.

“So the difference…”

He paused.

“…has always been grace.”

No one argued.

Because by now it was becoming clear.

Grace had not merely forgiven them.

Grace had **changed everything**.
