# Chapter 24

## The Cross Is Not Deceived by Carnality

The conversation continued the next evening.

But something had shifted.

Before, the men had been examining churches.

Now they were examining the cross.

Adrián spoke first.

“So yesterday we said something important.”

Matías nodded.

“The cross does not bow to carnality.”

“Yes.”

Adrián stirred his coffee slowly.

“And that explains why it makes people uncomfortable.”

Elías looked up from the Bible.

“Why.”

“Because it removes every human advantage.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“No pride.”

“No accomplishment.”

“No system strong enough to stand beside it.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“But there’s another thing we need to consider.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“How carnality survives.”

The others looked at him.

“What do you mean?” Marcos said.

Matías answered carefully.

“Carnality rarely presents itself honestly.”

The table grew quiet.

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That’s true.”

“No one says…”

He paused.

> “I would like to operate in the flesh today.”

The others laughed softly.

Matías continued.

“It usually sounds much better than that.”

Elías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“Much better.”

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“Give an example.”

Matías didn’t hesitate.

> “We need to stay united.”

The others recognized the phrase immediately.

Adrián nodded.

“That one shows up quickly.”

Elías added another.

> “Don’t cause division.”

Marcos spoke next.

> “Stop stirring things up.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

> “Just leave it alone.”

They sat quietly for a moment.

Matías spoke again.

“None of those sentences sound wrong.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“In fact they sound responsible.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“But sometimes they protect the wrong thing.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“What.”

“Systems.”

“Leaders.”

“Traditions.”

The words settled on the table.

Marcos leaned back slightly.

“That’s where the cross becomes uncomfortable.”

“Why?” Adrián asked.

“Because the cross sees through those disguises.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“The cross is not impressed by spiritual language.”

Adrián smiled.

“That sounds blunt.”

“It is,” Marcos said.

“Paul says something similar in Corinthians.”

He opened the Bible again.

“Listen.”

He read slowly.

> “For the word of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing…”

He paused.

“…but to us who are being saved it is the power of God.”

Adrián nodded.

“We read that yesterday.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“But Paul keeps going.”

He read again.

> “Where is the wise man? Where is the scribe? Where is the debater of this age?”

The others listened carefully.

Marcos finished the thought.

> “Has not God made foolish the wisdom of the world?”

Adrián leaned forward.

“So the cross exposes something.”

“Yes.”

“What.”

“Human wisdom.”

Elías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“The ways we justify ourselves.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“And the ways we protect ourselves.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That explains something else.”

“What,” Marcos asked.

“Why people sometimes resist the message of grace.”

The others looked at him.

Adrián continued.

“They say things like…”

He paused and quoted a phrase they all knew.

> “Grace is nice, but you can’t be without the law.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“I’ve heard that sentence many times.”

Elías added another.

> “All you talk about is grace.”

Marcos spoke next.

> “People will take advantage.”

Adrián smiled again.

“That one shows up immediately.”

The table sat quietly.

Matías spoke carefully.

“The concern behind those statements is not always wrong.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“But sometimes it hides something else.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Fear.”

Elías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Fear of losing control.”

The words were simple.

But they landed heavily.

Adrián leaned back.

“That makes sense.”

“What does?” Matías asked.

“If a church is built around managing carnality…”

He gestured slightly.

“…then grace sounds dangerous.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Because grace removes the controls.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“And the cross removes the pride.”

Elías added softly.

“And the cross exposes the flesh.”

The table grew quiet again.

Adrián finally said something almost thoughtfully.

“You know what I’m starting to notice.”

“What,” Marcos asked.

“Carnality is clever.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“It knows how to survive.”

“How?” Adrián asked.

“By sounding spiritual.”

The café noise filled the silence for a moment.

Then Marcos spoke again.

“That’s exactly why Paul refused to move away from the cross.”

The others looked at him.

He continued calmly.

“The cross cuts through every disguise.”

“Pride.”

“Effort.”

“Control.”

“Fear.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“So the cross is not deceived by carnality.”

“No,” Elías said.

“It sees it clearly.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“And that means something important.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“If a church truly centers itself on the cross…”

He paused.

“…then every disguise will eventually be exposed.”

No one spoke.

Because they all knew what that meant.

The cross does not merely forgive sin.

It reveals the flesh.

And when the flesh is revealed…

no system can protect it.

Adrián finally broke the silence.

“Well.”

He smiled faintly.

“That explains why the cross is rarely the center of church strategy meetings.”

No one laughed.

Because the thought was too accurate.
