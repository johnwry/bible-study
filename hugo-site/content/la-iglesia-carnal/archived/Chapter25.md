# Chapter 25

## The Cross Confronts Carnality

The next evening felt quieter than the others.

Not because the café was empty.

But because the conversation at the table had become more serious.

For several days they had been following a trail through Scripture.

Corinth.

Galatia.

Ephesus.

Laodicea.

Then the works of the flesh.

Then the cross.

Adrián finally spoke.

“So far we’ve said two things.”

Matías nodded.

“The cross does not bow to carnality.”

“And it is not deceived by it,” Elías added.

“Yes.”

Adrián looked at the open Bible.

“But that still leaves a question.”

“What question?” Marcos asked.

“What the cross actually does to it.”

The others waited.

Adrián continued.

“It exposes the flesh.”

“Yes.”

“But does it do anything beyond that?”

Marcos turned a few pages quietly.

“There is a passage that answers that directly.”

“Where,” Matías asked.

“Galatians again.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That letter is becoming important.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Very.”

He began reading slowly.

> “Now those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires.”

The words sat heavily on the table.

Adrián spoke first.

“Crucified again.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“That language keeps appearing.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“That sounds decisive.”

Elías nodded.

“Crucifixion always is.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“But wait.”

He looked at the verse again.

“That says something important.”

“What,” Marcos asked.

“It says those who belong to Christ…”

“…have crucified the flesh.”

“Yes.”

“That sounds like something already accomplished.”

Marcos nodded.

“It does.”

Matías spoke slowly.

“So the cross does not merely confront carnality…”

“…it declares its end.”

No one spoke for a moment.

Because that thought was far more radical than anything they had discussed yet.

Adrián finally said it.

“That’s not how most churches talk about it.”

“No,” Elías said quietly.

“What do they say?” Marcos asked.

Adrián answered without hesitation.

“They say we must constantly fight the flesh.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“That’s the language people use.”

“Fight it.”

“Resist it.”

“Control it.”

Marcos looked down at the passage again.

“But Paul says something different.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“He says the flesh has been crucified.”

The table fell quiet.

Matías spoke carefully.

“That changes the entire picture.”

“How,” Adrián asked.

“If the flesh is already judged…”

He paused.

“…then the Christian life is not about improving it.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“It is about recognizing what the cross has already done to it.”

Elías leaned back slightly.

“That would explain something.”

“What,” Matías asked.

“Why Paul never tries to train the flesh.”

Adrián looked puzzled.

“What do you mean?”

“In every letter…”

Elías continued,

“…Paul exposes the flesh.”

“He warns about it.”

“But he never tries to reform it.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Because the cross has already pronounced its verdict.”

Adrián leaned back.

“So the cross confronts carnality by declaring it finished.”

“Yes.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“That’s very different from carnality management.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“Carnality management assumes the flesh must be controlled.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“While the cross assumes it has already been condemned.”

The words settled heavily.

Elías spoke slowly.

“That may be why the message of the cross always sounds extreme.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“Because it removes the entire middle ground.”

Matías looked thoughtful.

“No negotiation.”

“No improvement.”

“No compromise.”

Marcos nodded.

“Just crucifixion.”

The café noise continued around them.

But the table had grown very still.

Adrián finally spoke again.

“You know what this explains.”

“What,” Elías asked.

“Why people resist this message.”

“Why?”

“Because it leaves no place for pride.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“And no place for human achievement.”

Marcos added quietly,

“And no place for the flesh to survive.”

The table fell quiet once more.

After a moment Adrián spoke again.

“So if the cross confronts carnality like this…”

He paused.

“…then the real question is not how to manage the flesh.”

The others looked at him.

Adrián finished the thought calmly.

“The real question is whether we believe what the cross has already done to it.”

No one answered immediately.

Because that question was no longer theoretical.

It was personal.
