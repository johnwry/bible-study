# Chapter 4

## Tracing the Flesh

The café looked the same as always.

Same tables.

Same smell of coffee.

Same quiet noise of people talking.

But the conversation at the table by the window felt different now.

The question from the previous week had stayed with them.

Adrián arrived first again.

He had already finished half his coffee by the time Elías walked in.

“You started without me.”

“I always start without you.”

Elías sat down.

“Well?”

“Well what?”

“You said you were going to look into it.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“I did.”

“And?”

Adrián leaned back in his chair.

“I’m confused.”

Elías smiled slightly.

“That didn’t take long.”

Adrián pointed at him.

“No, listen.”

He leaned forward again.

“I thought this was going to be simple.”

“What did you expect?”

“I expected to find a few verses about people doing bad things.”

“And?”

“And that’s not what I found.”

Elías waited.

“What did you find?”

Adrián rubbed the back of his neck.

“Honestly?”

“Yes.”

“I have more questions now than when we started.”

Just then Marcos arrived and sat down.

“More questions about what?”

Adrián pointed at him.

“Perfect timing.”

“What did I miss?”

“We’re talking about the flesh.”

Marcos nodded.

“Ah.”

“You looked into it too?”

“A little.”

“And?”

Marcos shrugged.

“Same problem.”

Adrián pointed at him again.

“See?”

“What problem?”

“The word shows up in places I didn’t expect.”

Matías arrived a few minutes later and joined them.

“You started already.”

“Yes,” Adrián said.

“Good.”

Matías sat down.

“What did you find?”

Adrián laughed quietly.

“That’s the problem.”

“What.”

“I’m not sure what I found.”

Elías leaned forward slightly.

“Start somewhere.”

Adrián nodded.

“Alright.”

He reached into his bag and pulled out a small notebook.

“I started by looking for the word flesh in the letters.”

“Paul’s letters?” Marcos asked.

“Yes.”

“And?”

Adrián flipped a few pages.

“The first thing I noticed is that Paul talks about the flesh a lot.”

“That doesn’t surprise me,” Matías said.

“Wait,” Adrián said.

“It gets stranger.”

“How.”

“In some places it clearly refers to sinful behavior.”

“That’s what we expected,” Marcos said.

“Yes.”

“But in other places…”

He looked up.

“It doesn’t.”

The others waited.

Elías finally said,

“Give an example.”

Adrián flipped another page.

“Galatians.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“That makes sense.”

“Yes,” Adrián said.

“But listen to this.”

He turned the notebook toward them.

“Paul says something strange.”

“What.”

Adrián read slowly.

> “Having begun by the Spirit, are you now being perfected by the flesh?”

Matías frowned immediately.

“That’s Galatians 3.”

“Yes.”

“What’s strange about it?” Marcos asked.

Adrián looked up.

“Think about it.”

“They began by the Spirit.”

“Yes.”

“But now they’re trying to finish by the flesh.”

“Yes.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“That sounds like effort.”

“Yes,” Adrián said.

“Religious effort.”

The table went quiet.

Marcos tapped the table slowly.

“So the flesh might not just mean immoral behavior.”

“No,” Elías said.

“It might also mean relying on human strength.”

Matías frowned more deeply.

“That’s not necessarily wrong.”

“What.”

“Effort.”

Marcos looked at him.

“But Paul seems to be criticizing it.”

Matías didn’t answer immediately.

Adrián continued.

“That’s what confused me.”

He flipped another page.

“And then I saw something else.”

“What.”

“The works of the flesh.”

“Galatians 5,” Elías said.

“Yes.”

Adrián nodded.

“And some of them are exactly what we expected.”

“Like what?” Marcos asked.

“Immorality.”

“That fits.”

“Yes.”

“But then there are others.”

“What.”

Adrián looked down at the list.

“Jealousy.”

Marcos nodded.

“That matches Corinth.”

“Yes.”

Adrián continued.

“Strife.”

“Also Corinth.”

“Factions.”

Matías looked uncomfortable.

“That’s interesting.”

Adrián closed the notebook slowly.

“Do you see the problem?”

“What problem?” Marcos asked.

Adrián looked around the table.

“If jealousy and strife are works of the flesh…”

He paused.

“Then the problem in Corinth wasn’t just bad behavior.”

“No,” Elías said quietly.

“It was something deeper.”

Matías stared at the table.

“So the flesh might not always look immoral.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“And it might not always look religious either.”

Adrián leaned back again.

“That’s exactly why I said I’m confused.”

He looked at the others.

“I thought the flesh meant obvious sin.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“So did I.”

Elías nodded slowly.

“But Scripture seems to be using the word differently.”

The table went silent again.

After a moment Adrián spoke.

“So now I have a question.”

“What.”

He looked around the table.

“If the flesh can look immoral…”

He paused.

“And it can also look religious…”

He leaned forward slightly.

“How would we recognize it?”

No one answered.

Because suddenly the investigation felt much closer to home than any of them expected.
