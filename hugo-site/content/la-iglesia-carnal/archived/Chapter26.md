# Chapter 26

## The Cross Disarms Carnality

The conversation did not begin immediately that evening.

For once Adrián was the quiet one.

He sat staring into his coffee as the others arrived.

Matías noticed it first.

“You look like you’ve been thinking.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That’s becoming a dangerous habit.”

Elías sat down and opened his Bible.

“What about?”

Adrián leaned back slowly.

“That verse from yesterday.”

“Which one?” Marcos asked.

> “Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“That one has been sitting with me too.”

Adrián rubbed his forehead lightly.

“I’ve spent most of my life thinking about the flesh as something that must constantly overpower me.”

The others listened quietly.

“So the Christian life became a kind of endless struggle.”

Matías spoke carefully.

“That’s how most people describe it.”

“Yes.”

Adrián nodded.

“Fight harder.”

“Try harder.”

“Be more disciplined.”

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“But Paul doesn’t describe it that way.”

“No,” Adrián said.

“That’s what’s bothering me.”

Elías turned to another passage.

“There’s something in Colossians that might help.”

Adrián smiled.

“There usually is.”

Elías read slowly.

> “When you were dead in your transgressions… God made you alive together with Him…”

He paused briefly.

“…having forgiven us all our transgressions.”

Matías nodded.

“That’s the gospel.”

“Yes.”

“But keep listening,” Elías said.

He continued reading.

> “Having canceled out the certificate of debt consisting of decrees against us…”

Adrián leaned forward slightly.

“That sounds like judgment being removed.”

“Yes.”

Elías finished the sentence.

> “He has taken it out of the way, having nailed it to the cross.”

The table was quiet.

Marcos spoke next.

“That’s decisive.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But the passage continues.”

He read the next line carefully.

> “When He had disarmed the rulers and authorities…”

Adrián blinked.

“Disarmed?”

“Yes.”

Elías continued.

> “He made a public display of them, having triumphed over them through Him.”

The words sat heavily on the table.

Matías spoke slowly.

“That language sounds like victory.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Something lost its power.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“So the cross doesn’t just expose carnality…”

“…it removes its authority.”

The others nodded.

Elías spoke quietly.

“That’s exactly what Paul is describing.”

Adrián thought for a moment.

“That explains something I’ve never understood.”

“What?” Matías asked.

“Why the New Testament keeps talking about freedom.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Freedom from sin.”

“Freedom from the law.”

“Freedom from fear.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“But if the flesh is still powerful…”

He gestured slightly.

“…that language wouldn’t make sense.”

Matías leaned back slowly.

“So the cross didn’t simply forgive us.”

“No,” Elías said.

“It disarmed what stood against us.”

The café noise filled the silence for a moment.

Then Marcos spoke again.

“That may explain why carnality often survives through intimidation.”

“What do you mean?” Adrián asked.

“Fear.”

“Pressure.”

“Condemnation.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Those things control people.”

Adrián thought about that.

“So if the cross removes condemnation…”

“…then it removes one of the flesh’s greatest weapons.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

Elías added quietly,

“And pride loses its ground as well.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Because everything becomes grace.”

The table grew quiet again.

Adrián finally spoke.

“So carnality is not as powerful as it appears.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“It survives mostly through deception.”

Elías looked down at the open Bible again.

“That’s why Paul keeps returning to the cross.”

“What do you mean?” Matías asked.

“Because the cross reminds believers what has already happened.”

Adrián leaned back.

“So the flesh shouts…”

“…but the cross has already spoken.”

The others sat quietly with that thought.

After a moment Matías spoke again.

“That’s very different from how churches often approach the problem.”

“How so?” Adrián asked.

“They act as if the flesh must constantly be restrained.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“More rules.”

“More pressure.”

“More systems.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“But the cross removes its weapons.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“And once its authority is gone…”

He paused.

“…the entire struggle begins to look different.”

The table fell quiet again.

Adrián finally said what they were all beginning to sense.

“If the cross has disarmed carnality…”

He looked around the table.

“…then maybe the Christian life isn’t about strengthening ourselves against the flesh.”

The others watched him.

Adrián finished the thought slowly.

“Maybe it’s about living in what the cross has already accomplished.”

No one rushed to answer.

Because for the first time since the investigation began…

the problem of carnality no longer felt overwhelming.

It felt… defeated.
