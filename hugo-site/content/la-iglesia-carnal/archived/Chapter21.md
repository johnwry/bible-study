# Chapter 21

## When Carnality Tries to Cure Carnality

No one spoke for a while.

The question was still sitting there.

If managing carnality doesn’t solve it…
 what does?

Adrián finally broke the silence.

“Well,” he said slowly, “churches do try to deal with it.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“They discipline people.”

“They correct people.”

“They tighten standards.”

Marcos leaned back slightly.

“That’s true.”

Adrián shrugged.

“That seems reasonable.”

“Does it?” Elías asked.

Adrián looked at him.

“What do you mean?”

Elías spoke carefully.

“Sometimes it helps.”

“But sometimes it makes things worse.”

Matías frowned slightly.

“How.”

“Think about it.”

“When someone falls into sin…”

“Yes,” Matías said.

“The church reacts.”

“They bring correction.”

“They apply discipline.”

“That’s biblical,” Matías added.

“Yes,” Elías said.

“No one is arguing with that.”

Marcos spoke next.

“But what happens if the discipline itself comes from the flesh?”

No one answered immediately.

Adrián leaned forward.

“What would that even look like?”

Marcos answered slowly.

“Anger.”

“Pride.”

“Humiliation.”

“Power.”

The table grew quiet.

Matías spoke softly.

“I’ve seen that.”

Adrián nodded.

“So have I.”

Elías added,

“And when discipline comes from the flesh…”

He paused.

“…what does it produce?”

Marcos answered.

“More flesh.”

No one smiled.

Because they had all seen it.

A person falls.

Leaders react strongly.

People take sides.

Some defend the leaders.

Others defend the person.

Soon the church is full of tension.

And the original problem multiplies.

Adrián spoke quietly.

“I’ve watched entire churches split over situations like that.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Instead of restoration…”

“…you get factions.”

Marcos spoke again.

“That’s exactly what Paul listed in Galatians.”

Strife.

Dissensions.

Factions.

Adrián leaned back.

“So carnality reacting to carnality…”

“…creates more carnality.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

Matías thought about it.

“That explains something.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Why so many church disciplines end badly.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“Because the goal quietly shifts.”

“What goal?” Adrián asked.

“Protecting the system.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“That’s uncomfortable.”

Elías added quietly,

“And protecting leaders.”

No one argued.

Matías spoke carefully.

“I’ve heard people say things like…”

He hesitated slightly.

Then finished the thought.

> “We need to protect the testimony of the church.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“That phrase shows up quickly.”

Marcos added another one.

> “We can’t let this spread.”

Elías said another.

> “If we don’t deal with this firmly the whole church will suffer.”

Matías nodded.

“And sometimes that concern is real.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“But if the response comes from fear…”

“…or anger…”

“…or pride…”

He paused.

“…then the cure becomes part of the disease.”

The table went quiet again.

Adrián finally said it plainly.

“So carnality cannot cure carnality.”

“No,” Elías said.

“It only multiplies it.”

Matías spoke slowly.

“That would explain something else.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Why so many churches become harder over time.”

“Harder how?”

“More rules.”

“More caution.”

“More pressure.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“They start building systems to keep people in line.”

Marcos smiled faintly.

“And eventually those systems become the identity of the church.”

Elías added quietly,

“And questioning them becomes dangerous.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Yes.”

He raised a finger slightly.

> “Don’t be rebellious.”

The others recognized the line immediately.

Matías added another.

> “You’re dividing the church.”

Marcos added another.

> “Stop causing waves.”

Elías finished the pattern.

> “We need to stay united.”

They sat with that for a moment.

Adrián finally spoke again.

“You know what’s strange?”

“What,” Matías asked.

“All of those sentences sound spiritual.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“But they’re often protecting something very fragile.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

Marcos answered plainly.

“A system that only works if people remain controlled.”

Matías looked down at the table again.

“That’s difficult to hear.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But if Paul is right…”

He looked back toward the open Bible.

“…then the flesh cannot produce the Christian life.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“So trying harder doesn’t solve it.”

“No.”

“More rules don’t solve it.”

“No.”

“More pressure doesn’t solve it.”

“No.”

The table sat in silence again.

Finally Adrián said what everyone was starting to feel.

“So if the flesh can’t fix the flesh…”

He looked around the table.

“…then what can?”

No one answered.

But this time the silence felt different.

Because the investigation had now closed every door they usually trusted.

Effort.

Discipline.

Control.

Management.

None of them had solved the problem.

And that meant something else had to exist.

Something deeper.

Something powerful enough to deal with the flesh itself.

Marcos looked down at the Bible again.

Then he said quietly,

“There is one thing Paul keeps returning to.”

The others looked at him.

“What,” Adrián asked.

Marcos answered simply.

“The cross.”
