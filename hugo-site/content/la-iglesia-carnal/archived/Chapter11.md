# Chapter 11

## The Divided Church

Elías had not moved the Bible.

It was still open in front of him.

Adrián leaned forward again.

“Keep going.”

“What.”

“Corinth.”

“You want more?”

“Yes.”

“If we’re going to compare it to churches we’ve seen…”

He gestured toward the Bible.

“We should probably see the whole picture.”

Elías nodded.

“That’s fair.”

He flipped back a page.

“Let’s start earlier.”

“Earlier?” Marcos asked.

“Yes.”

“This is near the beginning of the letter.”

He read slowly.

> “Now I exhort you, brethren, by the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that you all agree…”

Matías nodded.

“That sounds good.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But listen to the rest.”

He continued reading.

> “That there be no divisions among you…”

Adrián sat up.

“Divisions.”

“Yes.”

Elías continued.

> “But that you be made complete in the same mind and the same judgment.”

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“Why would Paul say that unless there were divisions?”

“Exactly,” Elías said.

He read the next line.

> “For I have been informed concerning you, my brethren, that there are quarrels among you.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Well that escalated quickly.”

Matías frowned.

“Quarrels.”

“Yes.”

Elías kept reading.

> “Now I mean this, that each one of you is saying, ‘I am of Paul,’ and ‘I of Apollos,’ and ‘I of Cephas,’ and ‘I of Christ.’”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“There it is.”

“What,” Adrián said.

“Factions.”

Matías nodded.

“Groups forming around leaders.”

“Yes.”

Adrián leaned back.

“That sounds… familiar.”

Marcos smiled slightly.

“It should.”

Elías closed the Bible for a moment.

“Think about what’s happening.”

“What.”

“These believers are organizing themselves around different teachers.”

Adrián shrugged.

“Is that necessarily wrong?”

Matías answered.

“It becomes wrong when it divides the church.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

Marcos added,

“When loyalty to a leader becomes stronger than unity in Christ.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“That happens more than people admit.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

Marcos leaned back.

“You know something.”

“What,” Adrián said.

“We usually think of division as a personality problem.”

“What do you mean?”

“People just not getting along.”

“Yes.”

“But Paul calls it something else.”

“What.”

Elías answered quietly.

“Flesh.”

The word landed heavily.

Adrián looked at the Bible again.

“So division…”

He paused.

“…is evidence of carnality.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

Matías spoke slowly.

“That changes things.”

“How.”

“If jealousy, strife, and factions are works of the flesh…”

He pointed at the table.

“Then division in a church is not a small issue.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“It reveals the source.”

Adrián leaned forward again.

“So let me see if I understand this.”

“What,” Marcos said.

“If we walked into a church and saw groups competing…”

“Yes.”

“People aligning with different leaders…”

“Yes.”

“Tension under the surface…”

“Yes.”

Adrián looked around the table.

“That might be evidence of the flesh.”

Elías nodded.

“That’s what Paul is saying.”

Matías looked thoughtful.

“And the church in Corinth still had gifts.”

“Yes.”

“And activity.”

“Yes.”

“And excitement.”

“Yes.”

Adrián shook his head slowly.

“So none of those things proved they were spiritual.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“They only proved they were active.”

The table went quiet again.

Finally Adrián said,

“You know what this means.”

“What,” Elías asked.

“It means a church might look very impressive…”

He gestured slightly.

“…while something much deeper is wrong.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“Yes.”

“And that problem might not be visible immediately.”

Marcos nodded.

“But the fruit will eventually reveal it.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“Well.”

He took another sip of coffee.

“I think I’m starting to understand why Paul called them carnal.”

Elías reopened the Bible.

“Yes.”

“But we’ve only looked at one church so far.”

Marcos nodded.

“That’s true.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“What do you mean.”

Marcos answered.

“We should probably compare the others.”

“The others?”

“Yes.”

He looked around the table.

“The churches we visited.”

Adrián smiled slowly.

“That sounds dangerous.”

“Why.”

“Because if this investigation keeps going…”

He paused.

“…we might start recognizing more of these patterns.”

Elías closed the Bible again.

“Yes.”

“And that might be exactly what we need to see.”
