 Chapter 22

## The Cross Paul Would Not Leave

For several moments no one spoke.

The word had been said almost quietly.

The cross.

Not loudly.

Not as a conclusion.

Just as an observation.

Marcos had not moved.

His hand still rested on the open Bible.

Adrián looked at him.

“You said Paul keeps returning to it.”

“Yes.”

“What do you mean?”

Marcos turned the Bible slightly so the others could see the page.

“Corinth.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That church again.”

“Yes.”

Marcos nodded.

“That church again.”

He pointed to the opening of the letter.

“When Paul began writing to them…”

He paused.

“…he did something interesting.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“What.”

“He didn’t begin by listing their problems.”

Elías nodded.

“That’s true.”

Marcos continued.

“He didn’t start with their divisions.”

“Or their pride.”

“Or their immorality.”

Adrián looked at the text.

“So where did he start?”

Marcos answered quietly.

“With the cross.”

The table sat quietly again.

Adrián spoke first.

“That seems obvious.”

“Does it?” Marcos asked.

Adrián shrugged.

“Well… that’s the gospel.”

“Yes.”

“But listen to how Paul says it.”

Marcos began reading slowly.

> “For the word of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing…”

He paused.

“…but to us who are being saved it is the power of God.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“I’ve heard that verse many times.”

Marcos looked up.

“So have I.”

“But I’m beginning to notice something.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“Paul doesn’t treat the cross as an introduction.”

The others watched him.

“What do you mean?” Elías asked.

Marcos answered carefully.

“He treats it as the center.”

He turned another page.

“And he keeps returning to it.”

Adrián leaned forward again.

“Show us.”

Marcos read again.

> “For indeed Jews ask for signs and Greeks search for wisdom…”

Then he finished the sentence.

> “But we preach Christ crucified.”

The words sat there quietly.

Adrián spoke slowly.

“That’s a strange message.”

“Why,” Matías asked.

“Because it doesn’t sound like a strategy.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“It isn’t a strategy.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“It’s a declaration.”

Marcos continued.

“Paul says something else right after that.”

He read again.

> “But to those who are called… Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“So the cross isn’t just forgiveness.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“It’s power.”

Matías looked thoughtful.

“Power for what?”

Marcos didn’t answer immediately.

Instead he turned a few pages.

“Listen to something Paul says later in the same letter.”

He read slowly.

> “For I determined to know nothing among you except Jesus Christ, and Him crucified.”

Adrián blinked.

“Nothing?”

“Yes.”

“That sounds extreme.”

Elías nodded.

“It does.”

Adrián looked puzzled.

“Why would Paul say that to a church full of problems?”

Matías answered quietly.

“Because he believed the cross dealt with the root.”

The table was silent again.

Marcos spoke slowly.

“We’ve spent the last few days asking how churches deal with carnality.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“Rules.”

“Pressure.”

“Management.”

“Systems.”

Marcos gestured toward the open Bible.

“But Paul keeps pointing to something else.”

“The cross.”

Matías leaned forward slightly.

“Are you saying the cross solves carnality?”

Marcos answered carefully.

“I’m saying Paul never leaves it.”

Elías looked down at the passage again.

“That’s true.”

“In every church we’ve discussed…”

He paused.

“…Paul keeps bringing them back to Christ crucified.”

Adrián sat back slowly.

“That’s interesting.”

“What,” Matías asked.

“All of our solutions have been about behavior.”

He gestured lightly.

“But Paul keeps pointing to something deeper.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“The source.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“If the flesh cannot please God…”

He looked at the others.

“…then the answer must come from somewhere outside the flesh.”

Matías looked at the page again.

“The cross.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“The cross.”

The café noise continued quietly around them.

Cups moving.

Low conversations.

Ordinary sounds.

But at the small table by the window, something had begun to settle in.

For years they had heard the message of the cross.

They had believed it.

They had preached it.

But mostly as the beginning of the Christian life.

Forgiveness.

Conversion.

Salvation.

And now they were beginning to see something they had overlooked.

Paul did not treat the cross as the beginning only.

He treated it as the center.

The power.

The answer to the very problem they had been trying to solve.

Adrián finally spoke.

“Maybe that’s why we’ve struggled.”

The others looked at him.

“What do you mean?” Matías asked.

Adrián answered slowly.

“Maybe we’ve been trying to solve carnality…”

He paused.

“…without understanding what the cross actually did.”

No one spoke for a moment.

Because that thought was both simple and enormous.

Marcos finally closed the Bible gently.

“If that’s true…”

He looked around the table.

“…then our investigation is about to change direction.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“How.”

Marcos answered calmly.

“We stop asking how to control the flesh.”

“And start asking what the cross actually did to it.”

The table fell quiet again.

Because that question…

was about to open the most important part of the investigation yet.
