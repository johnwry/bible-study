# Chapter 20

## Carnality Management

The question stayed on the table longer than any of them expected.

Why do churches spend so much time trying to manage the works of the flesh instead of confronting them?

No one answered right away.

Adrián was the first to speak.

“Maybe because they don’t know what else to do.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“That’s probably true.”

Matías leaned back.

“Or maybe because they’re trying to protect the church.”

Elías looked at him.

“Protect it from what?”

“Chaos.”

“Division.”

“People doing whatever they want.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That sounds noble.”

“It is noble,” Matías said.

“At least the concern is.”

No one argued with that.

Marcos finally spoke.

“But that still doesn’t answer the question.”

“What question,” Adrián asked.

“Why churches seem so prepared to live with carnality.”

That sat there.

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“You mean like… expecting it?”

“Yes.”

“Planning around it.”

“Building systems for it.”

The table was quiet again.

Then Matías said something very familiar.

“Well, all churches have leaders that are a little legalistic.”

No one said anything for a moment.

Adrián looked at him.

“That’s exactly the kind of sentence people say.”

Matías frowned.

“What.”

“As if that explains everything.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“As if a little flesh is just part of the furniture.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“Maybe that’s exactly the problem.”

They all looked at him.

“What do you mean,” Adrián asked.

Elías folded his hands.

“Maybe we’ve accepted carnality as normal.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“That’s uncomfortable.”

“Is it false?” Elías asked.

No one answered immediately.

Because they had all heard the language.

We need to stay united.

Don’t cause waves.

That brother means well.

Yes, he’s strong, but he loves God.

He’s a little harsh, but he’s serious about holiness.

They sat with it.

Marcos broke the silence.

“I’ve heard people say things like that my whole life.”

“What things,” Adrián asked.

“That a little pressure is good for people.”

“That they need conviction.”

“That if you preach too much grace people will take advantage.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“That’s a real concern.”

Adrián looked at him.

“Is it?”

“Yes.”

“Why wouldn’t it be?”

Adrián leaned forward.

“Because people say that sentence all the time and never explain what they mean.”

Matías frowned.

“What sentence.”

Adrián answered immediately.

> ‘I believe in grace, but I’d never teach it that way because people would take advantage.’

The words landed hard because all four of them had heard them.

Marcos spoke quietly.

“Yes.”

“And usually right after that comes another one.”

“What.”

> ‘Where do you draw the line?’

Adrián laughed once.

“There it is.”

Matías didn’t laugh.

“That’s not an unreasonable question.”

“No,” Elías said.

“But it tells you something.”

“What.”

“That the whole way of thinking is still built around control.”

The table went quiet.

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“Managing outcomes.”

“Managing behavior.”

“Managing people.”

Adrián leaned back.

“So instead of dealing with the flesh…”

He paused.

“…we build rails.”

Matías spoke slowly.

“Rails matter.”

“Yes,” Adrián said.

“But rails aren’t life.”

That one sat there.

Elías finally spoke.

“Maybe that’s why so many churches feel heavy.”

No one answered.

Because they all knew what he meant.

Churches where people measured everything.

How often you showed up.
 How much you gave.
 How consistent your devotions were.
 Whether your children behaved properly.
 Whether your language sounded spiritual enough.
 Whether your questions were safe enough to ask.

Marcos spoke next.

“And if someone starts pushing on the system…”

He paused.

“…the phrases come out.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

He started counting them off lightly on his fingers.

> “Don’t be rebellious.”
>  “You’re dividing the church.”
>  “We need to stay united.”
>  “Just leave it alone.”
>  “They’re our brothers.”
>  “Stop causing waves.”

Matías looked down at the table.

“That does happen.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“And the language sounds spiritual.”

Marcos nodded.

“But often it just means…”

He finished the thought plainly.

> “Don’t disturb the arrangement.”

No one smiled.

Because that felt too close.

Adrián broke the silence again.

“You know another one?”

“What,” Marcos asked.

> “That’s hyper-grace.”

He said it with just enough irony to make the others recognize it immediately.

Matías exhaled through his nose.

“Yes.”

“That one too.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“No definition.”

“No explanation.”

“Just a label.”

Elías added quietly,

“The same with antinomian.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“People use the word like a hammer.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“And if you ask what they mean…”

He lifted both hands slightly.

“Nothing.”

Matías finally spoke.

“Not always nothing.”

The others looked at him.

He continued carefully.

“Sometimes they mean…”

He paused.

“They’re afraid people will stop taking obedience seriously.”

Elías nodded.

“That fear is real.”

“Yes.”

“But fear can still organize a church around the wrong thing.”

The words sat heavily over the table.

Marcos spoke next.

“Maybe that’s what carnality management really is.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

Marcos answered slowly.

“It’s when the church stops expecting real freedom…”

…and starts trying to keep the flesh under control.

No one interrupted him.

He continued.

“Not cured.”

“Not confronted at the root.”

“Just managed.”

Adrián rubbed his forehead.

“That explains a lot.”

“What,” Matías asked.

“Why so many churches can function for years with the same tensions.”

“The same factions.”

“The same pressure.”

“The same fear.”

He looked around the table.

“They’re not solving the problem.”

“They’re learning to live with it.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“And sometimes to protect it.”

The others looked at him.

Elías continued.

“If a system is built around controlling carnality…”

He paused.

“…then that system actually depends on carnality continuing.”

No one spoke.

Because the thought was too sharp to move past quickly.

Marcos finally said it out loud.

“So if people were actually free…”

He leaned forward slightly.

“…what would happen to half the systems we’ve built?”

Adrián laughed once, but there was no humor in it.

“They’d collapse.”

Matías stared at the table.

“That’s hard to hear.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But it may be true.”

Adrián spoke more quietly now.

“Maybe that’s why so many churches don’t know what to do with grace.”

No one answered.

He kept going.

“Grace sounds too dangerous.”

“Too free.”

“Too open.”

“So people say things like…”

He looked around the table and repeated the line almost wearily.

> “Grace is nice, but you can’t be without the law.”

Matías closed his eyes for a moment.

He had heard that sentence too many times.

Marcos spoke carefully.

“And then another system gets built.”

“What kind of system,” Adrián asked.

“One that can survive as long as people keep failing.”

The café noise continued around them.

A cup dropped near the counter.

Someone laughed.

The ordinary world kept moving.

But at the table by the window, something had become painfully clear.

Carnality management was not merely a mistake.

It was a way of arranging church life so that the flesh remained predictable, controllable, and useful.

Adrián finally said what all of them were thinking.

“So let me ask the obvious question.”

The others looked at him.

“If managing carnality doesn’t solve it…”

He paused.

“…what does?”

No one answered.

Not because the question was unclear.

But because all four of them knew they had just reached the edge of something much bigger than church strategy.

And whatever the answer was…

it would have to go deeper than rules, pressure, labels, or control.

It would have to deal with the flesh itself.
