# Chapter 10

## The Corinth Question

Elías rested his hand on the open Bible.

Adrián watched him with a suspicious smile.

“You look too calm,” Adrián said.

“Why.”

“That’s the face you make right before you ruin something.”

Marcos laughed quietly.

“He might.”

Matías leaned forward.

“Let’s just read it.”

Elías nodded.

“Alright.”

He turned a few pages carefully.

“First Corinthians.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“So what exactly was going on in Corinth?”

Elías began reading slowly.

> “Now concerning spiritual gifts, brethren, I do not want you to be unaware…”

Adrián sat up.

“Wait.”

“What.”

“Spiritual gifts?”

“Yes.”

Adrián smiled.

“Well that already sounds familiar.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

Elías continued reading portions of the letter, moving through several passages.

Paul describing the gifts.

Prophecy.

Tongues.

Knowledge.

Healing.

Adrián shook his head slightly.

“They had everything.”

“That’s what it sounds like,” Marcos said.

Elías turned another page.

“And listen to this.”

He read again.

> “You are not lacking in any gift…”

Adrián leaned back slowly.

“That sounds exactly like the church I visited.”

Matías nodded thoughtfully.

“A gifted church.”

“Yes.”

Elías looked up.

“But Paul still corrects them.”

“For what,” Adrián asked.

Elías flipped back a few pages.

“This.”

He pointed to the passage they had read earlier.

> “For since there is jealousy and strife among you, are you not fleshly?”

No one spoke.

Adrián finally said,

“That’s the same passage we looked at.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But now we’re seeing the context.”

Marcos leaned forward.

“They had gifts.”

“Yes.”

“They had activity.”

“Yes.”

“They had people speaking.”

“Yes.”

“And yet Paul still calls them carnal.”

“Yes.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“That’s strange.”

“Why,” Marcos asked.

“Because when we think of a carnal church…”

He paused.

“We usually imagine the opposite.”

“What do you mean,” Matías said.

“A cold church.”

“A lifeless church.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“But Corinth wasn’t cold.”

“No,” Elías said.

“They were energetic.”

“Very.”

Adrián looked thoughtful.

“So a church can look very alive…”

He gestured toward the Bible.

“And still be carnal.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“That seems to be exactly what Paul is saying.”

Elías nodded.

“Yes.”

Marcos leaned back.

“That’s important.”

“Why,” Adrián asked.

“Because it means activity alone proves nothing.”

Adrián smiled slightly.

“That might disappoint a lot of people.”

Matías looked serious.

“It should make us careful.”

Elías turned another page.

“There’s something else here.”

“What,” Marcos asked.

Elías read again.

> “Each one of you is saying, ‘I am of Paul,’ and ‘I of Apollos,’ and ‘I of Cephas.’”

Adrián looked confused.

“What does that mean?”

Marcos answered.

“Groups.”

“Different camps forming.”

Matías nodded.

“Factions.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“The very thing Galatians called a work of the flesh.”

Adrián leaned back again.

“So the fruit matches.”

“Yes.”

“The same fruit.”

Matías was quiet again.

Finally he said,

“That means something.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

Matías spoke slowly.

“It means carnality isn’t measured by how active a church is.”

“No,” Elías said.

“Or how gifted it is,” Marcos added.

Adrián finished the thought.

“It’s measured by the fruit.”

The table fell quiet again.

After a moment Adrián spoke softly.

“That’s uncomfortable.”

“Why,” Marcos asked.

“Because the church I visited felt great.”

He shrugged.

“And it might still be.”

“Yes,” Elías said.

“But feeling alive isn’t the same as being spiritual.”

Adrián laughed quietly.

“That’s a line right there.”

Matías leaned forward again.

“So if Corinth looked lively…”

“And still had jealousy and factions…”

He paused.

“Then recognizing carnality might be harder than we thought.”

No one disagreed.

Adrián finally lifted his cup again.

“Well.”

He smiled faintly.

“This investigation is starting to ruin my favorite churches.”

Marcos laughed.

“That might be the point.”

Elías closed the Bible slowly.

“Yes.”

Because if Corinth could look alive and still be carnal…

Then perhaps the real question was not which churches were lively.

But which churches were truly spiritual.

And that question had only just begun to unfold.
