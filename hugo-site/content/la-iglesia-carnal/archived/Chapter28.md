# Chapter 28

## Why Carnality Management Still Exists

The café was louder that night.

A group at the far table was laughing loudly.

Someone behind the counter dropped a tray.

But the four men at their usual table barely noticed.

Adrián was the first to speak.

“I keep coming back to the same question.”

Matías looked up.

“Which one?”

Adrián rested his elbows on the table.

“If the cross has confronted the flesh…”

“…and disarmed it…”

“…and if walking in the Spirit protects us from it…”

He paused.

“Then why do so many churches organize their entire life around controlling carnality?”

The others exchanged glances.

Because they all knew the answer might be uncomfortable.

Marcos finally spoke.

“Maybe because people don’t believe the cross has really dealt with it.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“If you assume the flesh is still the main power in the Christian life…”

“…then you have no choice but to manage it.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“That sounds familiar.”

Elías looked at him.

“What do you mean?”

“I’ve heard statements like that my whole life.”

He began counting on his fingers.

“I believe in grace…”

He paused.

“…but I’d never teach it.”

The others nodded immediately.

Adrián continued.

“People would take advantage.”

Matías leaned back slightly.

“Yes.”

“That’s a very common fear.”

Marcos added quietly,

“It assumes the flesh must constantly be restrained.”

Adrián nodded.

“And then someone inevitably asks the next question.”

“What question?” Elías asked.

Adrián smiled.

“Where do you draw the line?”

The table grew quiet.

Matías shook his head slightly.

“That phrase alone has shaped entire church cultures.”

“How so?” Marcos asked.

“Because the secret genius of leadership…”

Matías paused.

“…is assumed to be knowing where to draw the line.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Yes.”

“Just enough rules to keep people in check.”

Marcos looked thoughtful.

“But the cross doesn’t operate that way.”

“No,” Elías said.

“The cross removes the entire system of line-drawing.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“And that makes people nervous.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“Yes.”

“Very nervous.”

Adrián continued.

“So what happens?”

“Churches build systems.”

“Rules.”

“Safeguards.”

“Accountability structures.”

Marcos nodded.

“Carnality management.”

Elías added quietly,

“And it often sounds very spiritual.”

Adrián smiled.

“Yes.”

“Grace is good…”

He raised an eyebrow.

“…but you can’t be without the law.”

Matías nodded slowly.

“I’ve heard that sentence many times.”

Marcos added,

“And then someone else says…”

“Grace, grace, grace.”

“All you talk about is grace.”

Adrián leaned back in his chair.

“Yes.”

“As if grace itself were the danger.”

The table fell quiet for a moment.

Matías spoke next.

“And when someone questions the system…”

Adrián finished the sentence.

“They’re accused of dividing the church.”

Elías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Unity must be protected.”

Marcos spoke carefully.

“But unity at the expense of truth…”

“…is not unity.”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“Try telling that to a church meeting.”

The others laughed quietly.

Matías spoke again.

“Sometimes the accusations become stronger.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

“That’s hyper-grace.”

Elías raised an eyebrow.

“And no one can define what that means.”

Marcos smiled.

“Or this one.”

“That’s antinomian.”

Matías chuckled softly.

“Another label.”

“But rarely explained.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“And when Scripture is brought up…”

He paused.

“…the response can become even more revealing.”

“What do you mean?” Marcos asked.

Adrián shrugged.

“I’ve actually heard someone say it.”

“What?”

Adrián shook his head slightly.

“I don’t care what the Bible says.”

The others looked at him.

Adrián finished the sentence quietly.

“This is how it’s always been.”

No one spoke for a moment.

Because they had all heard something similar.

Matías finally spoke.

“That may be the real reason carnality management survives.”

“How so?” Elías asked.

“Because systems feel safer than the cross.”

Marcos nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

“Systems give leaders control.”

Adrián added quietly,

“And they give the flesh somewhere to hide.”

The café noise continued around them.

But the table had grown very still again.

Adrián finally spoke.

“So maybe that’s the real issue.”

“What is?” Matías asked.

Adrián answered calmly.

“Carnality management exists…”

He paused.

“…because people don’t fully trust what the cross has already done.”

The others sat quietly with that thought.

Because it was becoming clearer with every conversation.

The cross had already spoken.

But many churches had built entire structures to avoid believing it.
