# Chapter 19

## The List They Didn’t Expect

The next evening the table filled quickly.

Adrián arrived first again.

But this time he didn’t stay long at the counter.

He came straight to the table.

That alone was unusual.

Matías noticed it immediately.

“You look serious.”

Adrián shrugged.

“Don’t get used to it.”

Elías opened his Bible before anyone else spoke.

“I kept thinking about Galatians.”

Marcos nodded.

“That makes sense.”

“If Paul confronted the Galatians for trying to continue by the flesh…”

He turned a few pages.

“…then we should probably read the passage where he actually describes it.”

Adrián leaned forward.

“You mean the famous one.”

“Yes.”

Matías nodded.

“The works of the flesh.”

Elías read slowly.

> “Now the deeds of the flesh are evident…”

Adrián smiled faintly.

“Well that should simplify things.”

Elías continued.

> “Immorality, impurity, sensuality…”

Adrián nodded.

“Corinth.”

“Yes,” Marcos said.

“That part fits easily.”

Elías kept reading.

> “Idolatry, sorcery…”

Matías shrugged.

“That’s less common in church.”

Adrián smiled.

“Hopefully.”

But Elías didn’t stop.

He read the next words carefully.

> “Enmity…”

He paused.

Then continued.

> “Strife…”

The table went quiet.

Elías read again.

> “Jealousy…”

Adrián slowly leaned back.

Elías continued.

> “Outbursts of anger…”

Matías looked thoughtful now.

Then Elías read the next line.

> “Disputes…”

And the next.

> “Dissensions…”

And finally.

> “Factions.”

No one spoke.

Because every one of them had heard those words before.

Just not in this list.

Adrián was the first to say it out loud.

“That sounds like Corinth.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

Jealousy.

Strife.

Factions.

Matías spoke slowly.

“That’s exactly what Paul confronted there.”

Elías looked down at the page again.

“But notice something.”

“What,” Adrián asked.

“These are not immoral behaviors.”

Adrián frowned.

“What do you mean?”

“They are relational.”

The table sat quietly.

Marcos spoke.

“Conflicts.”

“Divisions.”

“Group loyalties.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“That’s exactly what Corinth had.”

“Yes.”

Matías leaned forward.

“And what Galatia was heading toward.”

“Yes.”

Elías turned the page slightly.

“Paul actually lists the same pattern elsewhere.”

“Where,” Adrián asked.

Elías answered.

“First Corinthians.”

Matías nodded.

“Jealousy and strife.”

“Yes.”

Adrián rubbed his chin.

“So the works of the flesh are not just immoral sins.”

“No,” Marcos said.

“They include the kinds of conflicts churches constantly struggle with.”

The words sat heavily on the table.

Adrián spoke slowly.

“That changes how people think about carnality.”

Matías nodded.

“Yes.”

“Most people assume carnality means scandal.”

“But Paul seems more concerned about division.”

Elías spoke quietly.

“And pride.”

Marcos leaned forward slightly.

“So the flesh shows itself not only in obvious sin…”

“…but in the way people relate to one another.”

Adrián nodded slowly.

“That explains a lot.”

“What do you mean,” Matías asked.

“Church arguments.”

“Leadership conflicts.”

“Groups forming around personalities.”

He smiled faintly.

“Sound familiar?”

Marcos nodded.

“Very.”

Elías looked thoughtful.

“That might explain why Paul was so direct with Corinth.”

Adrián leaned back.

“Because they were calling something spiritual…”

He gestured slightly.

“…that Paul was calling flesh.”

Matías spoke quietly.

“That’s uncomfortable.”

“Why,” Adrián asked.

Matías answered carefully.

“Because most churches treat those kinds of conflicts as normal.”

Marcos nodded.

“Yes.”

“They manage them.”

Adrián laughed softly.

“Church politics.”

“Yes.”

Elías closed the Bible slowly.

“But Paul didn’t treat them as normal.”

“What did he call them?” Adrián asked.

Elías answered plainly.

“Works of the flesh.”

The table went quiet again.

Because something had now become painfully clear.

Carnality was not merely about scandal.

It was about something much closer to home.

Jealousy.

Strife.

Factions.

The very things churches often learn to tolerate.

Adrián finally spoke.

“So let me ask something.”

The others looked at him.

“If Paul already diagnosed the works of the flesh…”

He paused.

“…then why do churches spend so much time trying to manage them instead of confronting them?”

No one answered.

Because that question was about to open an entirely new part of the investigation.
