# Introduction

## A Question Worth Asking

There are realities within the modern church that are difficult to ignore. Respected leaders fall into patterns of sin that were hidden for years. Congregations preserve outward unity while relationships remain strained beneath the surface. Authority is sometimes guarded more firmly than transparency. Growth is measured, yet deeper health is often assumed rather than examined.

These observations are not offered as accusations, nor as commentary on isolated cases. They raise a more fundamental question: **How does the New Testament assess the condition of a church?** When Scripture speaks of believers or congregations as fleshly, what does it mean?

I do not begin this study with conclusions. I begin with examination. It is possible that I have misunderstood what spiritual health looks like. It is possible that I have overlooked how carefully the New Testament evaluates its churches. If Scripture applies the language of carnality with precision, then familiarity with the term is not enough. We must ask how it defines it.

------

## A Personal Catalyst

This inquiry became deeply personal over time.

For the past year, I have had the privilege of walking closely with young believers as we studied the gospel carefully — not as a slogan, but as Scripture presents it. Many wrestled with long-held assumptions. Many were profoundly changed. Some left established professions to pursue ministry with renewed clarity about Christ and His work.

When these young believers entered local churches for ministry practicum, their emphasis on the finished work of Christ did not always produce the response I expected. In some cases, it generated caution. In others, resistance. Labels were used where conversation might have been more fitting. Association alone sometimes resulted in distancing.

I recount this not to defend myself or them. Rather, it exposed a tension I had not fully considered. If clarity about the work of Christ unsettles rather than strengthens, then either I have misunderstood something, or we have. That possibility deserved careful examination.

Instead of defending a position, I chose to return to the New Testament with a question: When it describes believers or churches as fleshly, what does it mean? What does it confront? And how does it anchor correction?

------

## The Commitment

I do not write this book to prove a point. I write it to test one.

If the New Testament defines carnality with clarity, then our assumptions — including my own — must submit to that definition. If the person and work of Christ truly stand at the center of the church’s life, then every distortion will, in some way, reveal a departure from that center. Whether that is so must be determined not by experience, but by careful attention to Scripture.

This book is an invitation to that journey.

It begins with observation.
 It proceeds through examination.
 It allows patterns to emerge.
 Only then will conclusions be drawn.

If there is such a thing as a carnal church, then God has defined it. The task before us is not to speculate, but to listen.
