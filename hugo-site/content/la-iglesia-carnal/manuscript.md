# Prologue

Over the past several years I have watched something troubling unfold.

Young believers who had discovered the gospel with fresh clarity were sent to visit churches as part of their ministry training.

What happened next surprised all of us.

Some were welcomed.

But many were not.

They were told they spoke too much about grace.

That the gospel they shared sounded dangerous.

That it did not emphasize effort strongly enough.

Some were quietly warned.

Others were openly rejected.

A few were eventually pushed out of their churches entirely.

None of them had changed the message of Scripture.

They had simply begun to take it seriously.

Those events forced a question I could no longer ignore.

If the gospel is truly the power of God unto salvation, why does it sometimes feel unwelcome in the very place that claims to proclaim it?

That question began a journey.

The pages that follow are the record of that journey.

# What I Learned About the Carnal Church

For many evenings I sat nearby and listened.

The four men never noticed me.

They were too engaged with one another.

At first their conversations seemed ordinary. Friends meeting after their travels, sharing stories about churches they had visited.

But slowly the conversation changed.

What began as casual observations became an investigation.

One by one the churches were discussed.

Corinth.

Galatia.

Ephesus.

Laodicea.

At first the differences between them seemed obvious.

Corinth was openly troubled.

Galatia was intensely serious.

Ephesus was doctrinally careful.

Laodicea appeared strong and successful.

But as the conversation continued, something unexpected began to appear.

Underneath the differences, the same problem kept surfacing.

Carnality.

Not always obvious.

Not always scandalous.

Sometimes loud.

Sometimes respectable.

But always present.

At first the men tried to understand it through experience.

They spoke about churches.

About leadership.

About discipline.

About unity.

About success.

About failure.

They repeated phrases most of us have heard before.

“Where do you draw the line?”

“I believe in grace, but people will abuse it.”

“We must protect unity.”

“Grace is good, but you can’t be without the law.”

“We just need to try harder.”

All of it sounded familiar.

Because most churches have built their lives around managing carnality.

Systems are created.

Rules are added.

Pressure is applied.

The flesh is restrained, corrected, warned, and disciplined.

But rarely understood.

That was the turning point of the conversation.

When the men finally asked a simple question:

What does the Bible actually mean by “flesh”?

That question led them back to Scripture.

And once they began observing carefully, the picture became clearer.

Carnality is not limited to immorality.

Corinth proved that.

But neither is carnality limited to moral looseness.

Galatia proved that.

Carnality can also appear in devotion to doctrine.

Ephesus showed that.

And it can flourish inside religious success.

Laodicea demonstrated that.

Different expressions.

The same root.

The flesh.

For a time the conversation seemed stuck there.

If carnality could appear in so many forms, what hope was there for the church?

That is when the cross entered the discussion.

Not as a slogan.

Not as a theological statement.

But as the decisive act of God.

The cross does not negotiate with the flesh.

It does not reform it.

It does not manage it.

The cross condemns it.

Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires.

Once that truth became clear, many other passages suddenly made sense.

The cross confronts carnality.

The cross disarms it.

The cross protects the believer from it.

And grace, flowing from the cross, produces something entirely different.

Not managed carnality.

New life.

That is the difference the men slowly began to see.

The gospel is not merely forgiveness for failure.

It is the power of God unto salvation.

A salvation that reaches into daily life.

A salvation that produces fruit.

A salvation that leads believers to walk by the Spirit rather than constantly struggle with the flesh.

For years many churches have assumed that carnality is simply part of the Christian experience.

Something to manage.

Something to restrain.

Something to keep under control.

But the cross tells a different story.

The flesh has already been judged.

And grace has already been given.

The question is no longer whether the cross has spoken.

The question is whether we believe what it has said.

The four men eventually left the café that evening.

Their investigation had not produced a new system.

It had not produced a better strategy.

But it had revealed something far more important.

The answer had been present all along.

The cross.

And the grace that flows from it.

That grace has made all the difference.





I will end this with a question.

One that comes from years of struggle, frustration, and hurt.

I really tried.

For a long time I believed the answer was simply to try harder, to do better, to be more disciplined, more committed, more serious.

And I did try.

But after years of trying, I eventually had to face a painful truth.

I had failed.

Not once or twice, but again and again.

And yet, in that place of failure, God was gracious to meet me where I was.

Grace met me where I was.

It did not wait for me to measure up.

It did not demand that I first become what I could never make myself to be.

It met me there.

And that changed everything.

So let me ask you a simple question.

**If the cross has already judged the flesh… why are we still trying to manage it?**
