## 1. Adrián

A church that feels alive. Energy, participation, gifts on display, strong atmosphere. It feels right. Adrián leaves encouraged.

## 2. Elías

A church marked by seriousness, doctrinal care, and commitment to truth. Everything is correct, disciplined, and intentional. Yet something feels distant.

## 3. Marcos

A church that functions like a well-built system. Organization, outreach, media presence, expanding ministries. The work is impressive and efficient.

## 4. Matías

A church built around discipline, accountability, and moral seriousness. Commitment is measurable. Standards are high. Grace is acknowledged but guarded.

## 5. The Word We Keep Using

The four men meet again. Corinth and Galatia are compared. One question emerges:
 **How can two very different churches both be called “fleshly”?**

## 6. What Do We Mean by Flesh?

Each man describes what he thinks the flesh means. Their answers reveal their instincts, fears, and spiritual assumptions.

## 7. A Word Study Begins

Elías suggests they stop speculating and examine how Paul uses the word **flesh** with believers.

## 8. Corinth — Alive but Carnal

 Carnality can exist where spiritual activity is abundant.

## 9. Galatia — Serious but Carnal

Galatians 3 is explored. Moral seriousness, religious discipline, and cultural morality appear commendable — yet Paul calls it flesh.

## 10. The Strange Nature of the Flesh

A disturbing observation emerges:
 **The flesh can produce both immorality and strict morality.**

# PART I — THE MEN

## Adrián

Adrián knew within minutes whether a gathering carried weight.

It wasn’t about noise. It wasn’t even about size. It was about momentum — the sense that something was happening and everyone in the room knew it.

He stood near the back at first, scanning faces the way he always did. People were alert. Engaged. No one looked like they were waiting for it to be over.

He liked that.

When someone read from Scripture, the room leaned in. When someone spoke, others followed. There was no awkward hesitation, no fumbling transition. It moved.

That was the word.

It moved.

After the formal part ended, no one rushed for the door. Groups formed naturally. Conversations overlapped. Laughter came easily. A long table near the side wall filled quickly with food someone had brought in.

Adrián found himself pulled into three conversations in ten minutes.

A college student spoke about a recent decision to serve more intentionally. An older couple described how the church had “given them purpose again.” A young leader talked about vision — not abstractly, but with details.

There was confidence here.

Direction.

Clarity.

Gifts were evident — not spotlighted, but visible. Teaching flowed without strain. People spoke with assurance about their role. No one seemed unsure where they fit.

Adrián loved that.

He loved when a church felt like it knew itself.

Driving away, he replayed the morning in his mind.

Strong.

Alive.

Certain.

He smiled to himself.

This is how it should feel.

------

## Elías

Elías listened more than he spoke.

His upbringing had trained him to value careful thinking. Truth mattered. Precision mattered. Faithfulness was not measured by excitement but by endurance.

The church he visited impressed him in a different way.

There were no dramatic moments. No emotional peaks. The service moved steadily, thoughtfully.

Scripture was read carefully. The sermon was structured and clear. Doctrinal lines were drawn firmly.

He appreciated that.

Afterward, a younger speaker approached him with enthusiasm, explaining a point he had made in the sermon.

Elías listened patiently.

Then he asked a few questions.

Not to embarrass him.

To sharpen him.

Accuracy mattered.

Later, walking to his car, Elías felt a quiet satisfaction.

The church was serious.

Disciplined.

Faithful.

But even as he drove away, a strange thought lingered.

Everything had been correct.

But something felt distant.

------

## Marcos

Marcos noticed systems.

Where others saw atmosphere, he saw structure.

When the service began exactly on time, he noticed.

When the music moved smoothly from one piece to the next, he noticed.

When cameras moved quietly through the room broadcasting the gathering live, he noticed.

This church was organized.

Efficient.

Intentional.

After the service, one of the leaders explained their expanding outreach programs.

Food distribution. Counseling centers. New campuses being discussed.

Marcos nodded thoughtfully.

Structure that sustains.

He liked that phrase.

Driving away, he felt impressed.

Not emotionally stirred.

Impressed.

This church would still be standing in ten years.

------

## Matías

Matías valued seriousness.

The monument outside the church caught his attention immediately.

The Ten Commandments carved in stone.

Inside, similar reminders appeared on the walls.

Clear words.

Clear expectations.

The sermon was direct.

Grace was mentioned — but discipline was emphasized.

“We cannot drift into maturity,” the speaker said.

Matías nodded.

Afterward, small groups formed.

Members discussed their week.

One man admitted he had missed two mornings of prayer.

The group leader leaned forward.

“Then make tomorrow three hours.”

No one laughed.

Matías approved.

Commitment costs something.

Driving home, he felt steadied.

Better pressure than decay.

# PART II — THE INVESTIGATION

## The Word We Keep Using

The following week the four men found themselves at the same café again.

Adrián was already there when the others arrived. His Bible lay open in front of him.

Marcos pulled out a chair.

“Well?”

Matías leaned forward.

“How did your investigation go?”

Adrián exhaled.

“I’m confused.”

The others looked at him.

“I thought I was going to find a clear explanation,” he said.
 “But I have more questions than answers.”

Elías nodded slowly.

“That usually means you're getting closer to something.”

Adrián pushed the Bible toward them.

“I started with Corinth.”

He pointed.

> “I could not speak to you as to spiritual men, but as to fleshly…”

“They’re believers,” Adrián said.

“But Paul calls them fleshly.”

Matías nodded.

“Jealousy. Strife.”

“That part makes sense,” Adrián said.

He flipped back a page.

“But then this.”

> “You are not lacking in any gift.”

Marcos frowned.

“They have gifts.”

“Yes,” Adrián said.

“And Paul still calls them fleshly.”

He flipped forward again.

“And then there’s Galatians.”

Elías read the line.

> “Having begun by the Spirit, are you now being perfected by the flesh?”

The table fell quiet.

Adrián leaned back.

“Corinth looks chaotic.”

He tapped Galatians.

“Galatia looks disciplined.”

“But Paul uses the same word.”

Marcos spoke softly.

“The flesh.”

Adrián nodded.

“Yes.”

He looked around the table.

“What if we’ve been defining that word wrong all along?”

------

## What Do We Mean By Flesh?

The men sat quietly for a moment.

Finally Marcos spoke.

“What does that word actually mean?”

Adrián shrugged.

“To me it’s when people live however they want.”

He gestured toward Corinth.

“Jealousy. Pride. Immorality.”

Elías shook his head slightly.

“When I hear flesh, I think of believers drifting from truth.”

Matías leaned forward.

“To me it’s when obedience stops mattering.”

Marcos listened carefully.

Then he pointed to the text.

“But Paul seems to think people can sincerely try to obey…”

He tapped Galatians.

“…and still be operating in the flesh.”

The table went silent again.

Adrián finally spoke.

“Well.”

He looked around the table.

“That seems like a problem.”





# Investigation Question

How can such different environments all be described the same way?

Corinth — chaotic.
 Galatia — disciplined.

Yet Paul uses the same word.

The flesh.
